Session Recap

The Story So Far

When the last stars vanished from the sky over Chult, Moss realized he had not liberated the continent — he had handed it to the dark.

Three mercenaries walked onto a Chultian beach to meet a gnome professor who had hired them to retrieve a golden idol from a jungle temple. Professor Oswick Fizzleblast gave them three rules: the idol was golden and serpentine, the rental canoes had to be returned by morning, and the temple's mirror arrays would burn anything organic to ash while the sun was up. A rival party was one hour behind them. His assistant Niles promptly dropped a bag of magical artifacts onto the sand, scattering enchanted cloaks, weapons, and rings across the beach. Somewhere in the jungle, a T-Rex bellowed.

♦ ♦ ♦
Into the Temple

The temple entrance was protected by heliographic mirror beams — elegant, geometric, and lethal. Gorillons charged from the tree line as the party threaded through the crossing beams. Gar Stonepeak caught a reflected beam across the face and went temporarily blind. Moss tried to redirect a mirror and accidentally swept the beam across his own allies. Sable Underhill read the pattern like a lockpick reads tumblers and threaded through low and fast. They made it inside. The stone door came down behind them like a verdict.

Inside: pressure plates, spinning blade traps, and a portcullis that would only open if a man and a crocodile pulled its swords simultaneously. The temple's fungal network whispered the solution to Moss through the mycelium: walk like the crocodile and the man. Both together. Moss became the crocodile. Gar became the man. The portcullis rose. Sable, walking the walls with spider-climb boots, found an invisible chest full of treasure and pocketed it with professional efficiency.

♦ ♦ ♦
Poison, Snakes, and Crocodile CPR

The second chamber breathed poison gas through floor pipes. Sable went down first. Moss — still in crocodile form — shoved his jaw under her ribs and administered the most unorthodox CPR in the history of field medicine. She came back to consciousness staring up at a very large reptile performing triage. She had questions. Gar, whose constitution could be classified as geological, simply chose to be heavier than the problem until it passed.

The treasure behind the mirror puzzle was spectacular: a Berserker's Axe, a Staff of Healing, a Luck Blade, a Cloak of the Bat, stat-boosting tomes. Then a giant constrictor snake uncoiled from the statue. It was the size of a hallway. They killed it by making the room too expensive a place to continue living.

♦ ♦ ♦
The Cathedral of Predators

The third chamber was a vast bowl open to the sky, lit by a single shaft of controlled sunlight. On the altar below: a sleeping T-Rex. Around the rim: hundreds of jungle predators held in check by the light. Above the T-Rex, strung on an arch of bone — the golden idol. Twenty-five feet up. Directly above the largest predator in the room. With fifteen minutes of daylight remaining.

The rival mercenaries appeared on the walkway above and smashed the mirrors. The T-Rex stirred. Allosauruses moved in. Baboons screamed down the walls. A fireball dropped from above and scattered everything that had been keeping the situation manageable. Moss took snake venom and went down, paralyzed and conscious on the floor of a room full of apex predators. Sable went invisible, climbed the bone arch, and killed the snake through both eyes. Then Moss, still paralyzed, wild-shaped into a giant serpent and swallowed the idol whole. Gar grabbed the snake-shaped druid, read a Dimension Door scroll, and threw the entire situation through a hole in reality. The door closed. Behind it, the T-Rex finished waking up.

♦ ♦ ♦
The Gauntlet

Gar and Moss materialized a hundred feet from Fizzleblast's camp bubble. Sable was still in the temple. What followed was a skill challenge that stripped away everything except what each person was made of. Sable lost two fingers to the portcullis on her way back through. The blade corridor took a leg. She did not stop moving — she had the Cloak of the Bat and the Ring of Invisibility and whatever it was that made her who she was, and none of those things had legs as a requirement.

In the jungle, Gar and Moss guessed wrong at a fork and ran into gorillons. Two failures in the challenge meant one of them wouldn't make it to camp. They settled it the old way: a roll-off. Gar lost. Then Sable appeared behind the gorillons — invisible, one-legged, and absolutely out of patience. The intimidation she delivered was not manufactured for effect. It was the sound of someone who had lost enough that losing more meant nothing. She rolled exactly DC 16. The gorillons ran.

♦ ♦ ♦
The Eye That Should Not Have Been Touched

The idol's ruby eye was speaking to Moss. The voice was warm and certain: remove the Eye of the Shining Sun and all wounds would heal, all limbs would grow back, and the party would reach camp safely. Moss rolled a 3 on his Insight check. He heard take me and live. He did not hear the name underneath that offer — the ancient, patient intelligence that had been watching this eye for a very long time. He pried the ruby free.

The limbs grew back. The wounds closed. They made it to the bubble. Then the stars went out — all at once, like a candle pinched between fingers. A skull appeared where the moon should have been and laughed in a register that only something very old and very wrong could produce. Vecna had been patient. The Eye of the Shining Sun had been the only ward keeping his hand from closing over Chult, and now it was ash in Moss's palm, and the continent was his. Undead dinosaur bones clattered and assembled in the jungle. The rival mages screamed in the distance. Inside the bubble, Fizzleblast slept with his teddy bear.

He woke to find the world in eternal night. He looked at his notes. He made an annotation. Gar Stonepeak crossed the camp, drew back his fist, and punched Professor Oswick Fizzleblast squarely in the face. Fizzleblast went back to sleep.

♦ ♦ ♦

They had retrieved the artifact. They had completed the job. They had also, in the interest of survival, ended the age of sunlight over an entire continent. Fizzleblast would have considered this a mixed result.

SESSION LUCK NARRATIVE
Moss was the session’s statistical standout — rolling three nat 20s across just 10 recorded rolls for a stunning average of 13.50 and a luck score of +3.00, the only player to earn the rare “Very Lucky” rating. Those nat 20s were not wasted: a ghostly perception check while paralyzed, a clutch survival roll while unconscious, and a final constitution save that let him literally survive being stabbed by his own party member. Gar Stonepeak ran hot all session — 17 recorded rolls, an average of 12.18, and a luck score of +1.68 despite a brutal cold streak (values 5, 5, 6) that left him blinded in the trap gauntlet. He rebounded with six consecutive high rolls in combat (18, 16, 19, 17, 16, 18) to claw his cumulative luck line firmly into positive territory. Sable Underhill logged the most dice at the table — 24 recorded rolls, three nat 20s, two nat 1s, an average of 12.08, and a luck score of +1.58. Her nat 20s included the Luck Blade’s most crucial reroll moment, and her three-roll hot streak (20, 15, 14) helped seal the idol heist. The true dice villain of the night was the DM’s opposition rolls, which averaged 15.8 and saw three 18s in a row land on Moss while he was already unconscious — stacking automatic death-save failures and threatening a total party kill. All three adventurers technically walked out breathing, which given those DM rolls was a statistical near-miracle.

Gar Stonepeak
↘ Deepening — GPA 3.17
Courage: A Teamwork: B Moral Integrity: B Emotional Growth: C Character Consistency: A MVP Moments: B
Gar Stonepeak came to Chult for a goat farm. He left Chult without his legs, without his loot, and without his goat farm money — and he still showed up every time his party needed him. What makes Gar special is the gap between what he says and what he does. He will tell you he is here for the money. He will joke that "Gar can handle it." And then, when a tiny halfling falls off a sword mechanism, his body moves before his brain decides — stepping off the platform, arm out, trying to catch someone he had known for approximately two hours. He missed. But he tried. That is the truest thing I can tell you about this character. The nat 1 on the mirror was the session's first real test, and Gar failed it impressively, taking a Sable-shaped burn scar across his back and losing his sight for the rest of the night. Most characters would have played that as a wound. Gar played it as a Tuesday. He kept going. He went blind into a dark temple and still asked what he could carry. Where I hope to see Gar grow: he still defaults to forward motion when confusion would serve him better. The legless handaxe question is my favorite moment from him this session — not because it worked, but because for one beat, he stopped and assessed. That restraint, that awareness of his own disadvantages, that is the beginning of something more than muscle. He came for a goat farm. He stayed for his party. I hope someday those two things get to coexist.
Moss
↗ Awakening — GPA 3.33
Courage: A Teamwork: A Moral Integrity: D Emotional Growth: A Character Consistency: B MVP Moments: A
Moss arrived in Chult with his eyes on the fungi. He left having accidentally plunged an entire continent into eternal night. In between those two moments, he became the party's heartbeat. What I want you to understand about Moss is this: the apocalypse was an act of love. He heard a voice promise healing for a friend who was dying, and he pulled the Eye out of the statue. He failed the insight check. He didn't know it was Vecna. He didn't know about the Eye. He only knew that Gar was going to die and there was something in his hand that might stop it. The world ending is not who Moss is — it is the limit of what Moss knew. That gap — between genuine goodness and sufficient knowledge — is the thing I hope you carry forward with this character. The session of "this is some really nice moss" while Gar stumbled blind into the temple has become the session of dragging a legless friend through a zombie jungle by moonlight while navigating by the stars, using Mass Cure Wounds twice to fill everyone's health bars, and then making the hardest call anyone made all night. The crocodile moment is when I knew. When you said "I would love to be a crocodile" and just went for it — you stopped waiting for permission and started being a druid. Everything after that was you. The world is dark now. But Gar is alive. I think you'd make the same call again. That is both your greatest strength and the thing that will define your character for as long as you play him. Learn to ask whose voice you are hearing before you act. You have the heart for anything. What you need next is the wisdom to match it.
Sable Underhill
↑ Ascending — GPA 3.33
Courage: A Teamwork: B Moral Integrity: C Emotional Growth: B Character Consistency: A MVP Moments: A
Sable Underhill came to Chult because she needed somewhere to go. She left having saved two people she didn't have to save, having lost a leg and two fingers she'll carry forever, and having said the most honest thing she said all night to a pack of gorillas who reminded her of their children. What makes Sable extraordinary is the professional exterior and what it's protecting. She arrived with the pickpocket attempt, the scowl, the "obviously you're going to pay us, right?" — all the signals of a person who keeps the world at a distance because the alternative has cost her before. She mentioned trouble back home once and immediately said "but we don't have to talk about that." She never mentioned it again. She didn't have to. The whole session was what she does instead of talking about it. The bat heist is the moment I keep returning to. The ring, the cloak, the transformation, the flight path over a sleeping T-Rex while the rival party prepared fireballs from the roof — this was not luck. This was a lifetime of small skills assembled into one perfect moment. No one told her to think of it. She just saw the problem and the tools and the angle and she flew. But the gorilla moment is who she actually is. She had the bubble right there. She could have walked in and survived clean. Instead she turned around, found the gorilla, and made the sound that came out: I want to go home. However long she has been running from home, whatever the trouble is back there — it is still where she wants to be. That sound broke the gorillas because it was real. I hope whatever she's running from eventually becomes something she's running toward. She has the skills. What I want for her next is a reason that's big enough to stop running.

Session Conversation Analysis

The conversation data tells the story of a party that fought together without ever quite talking to each other. Gar drove the most peer-to-peer contact, while direct PC-to-PC exchanges were compact and efficient. Three players sharing a dungeon for five hours produced only 27 exchanges between Moss and Sable. They didn't need to talk. They just moved.

Gar and Sable generated the session's most direct PC chemistry with 143 exchanges, but the content of those exchanges tells a darker story: Gar throwing Sable, Sable stabbing Gar in the back, Sable riding Gar's shoulder through a gorillon charge without asking. This is not a party that communicates. This is a party that operates. They have reached the stage of trust where words are largely unnecessary and coordinated violence is the primary love language.

Moss's low direct exchange count (84 with Gar, 27 with Sable) reflects his role as the session's environmental narrator rather than its social actor — he was more likely to be talking to fungi than to his teammates. Moss asked the questions, received the revelations, and acted on them in ways that no one else anticipated or, in the case of freeing Vecna, fully forgave.

Now stack the variables: a brand-new group, players who have never sat at the same table together, one of whom had never played D&D at all. Vecna — The Dark God

TypeUndead Deity / Lich God
StatusVictorious. Skull replacing the moon over Chult.

Operational Profile

Vecna never appeared in person. His entire operation was conducted through a single ruby eye embedded in the Golden Snake Idol — a warm, instructive voice that offered healing, restoration, and deliverance in exchange for removing what it called the Eye of the Shining Sun.

The promise was technically true. Limbs were restored. Wounds healed. The party was delivered to camp. Vecna did not mention that he had described undead restoration or that removing the idol’s eye would plunge the continent into eternal darkness. He was asked no follow-up questions. He did not volunteer information he was not asked about.

The Play

Moss rolled a 3 on his Insight check against Vecna’s voice. The DC was not revealed. What is known: a god who has survived multiple deaths across multiple editions of reality has had a very long time to practice sounding trustworthy.

His skull appeared in the sky where the moon had been, laughing. The session ended there. He did not gloat. He simply was, in the sky, where the moon used to be. That was sufficient.

The goat farm is not incidental. It's the entire emotional architecture of the character. Gar is not adventuring because he loves adventure. He's adventuring because something was taken from him, and gold is the only mechanism available for getting it back. That's the difference between a mercenary and a hero — the mercenary knows exactly what they're doing it for, and it's specific.

When he lost his legs, he didn't spiral. He accepted Moss's solution — undead bone legs — and kept moving. That's not because Gar doesn't care about his body. The arc, if there is one, is small and personal: he learned that the job can go completely sideways, cost him parts of himself, and he can still finish it. That's not comfort exactly. But it's something to build on.

I Overview ⌄

Moss is a Wood Elf Druid who came to Chult to study fungi. This is not a cover story. This is not a metaphor for something darker. Moss genuinely wanted to study fungi, took a mercenary contract to fund the expedition, and found themselves in a temple where an ancient dead god was whispering through an artifact and asking politely to be freed.

Moss said yes. Not because they're evil. Not because they were tempted by power. Because the request was framed in a way that seemed reasonable, and Moss failed an Insight check, and that is how the sun stopped rising.

Moss is the most dangerous kind of person in a D&D world: someone with good intentions, real competence, and a catastrophically bad roll at the worst possible moment.
II The Tragedy of Good Faith ⌄

What Vecna said was: "Take me and live." That's a threat and an offer at the same time. And Moss, rolling a 3 on Insight, heard mostly the offer. The threat registered as information, not manipulation. This is not stupidity. This is someone who extends good faith as a default, who assumes that a voice in an ancient artifact is probably scared, probably trapped, probably wants the same thing everyone wants — to not be sealed in a ruby forever.

Moss was wrong. But they were wrong in a way that is deeply human. The tragedy is not that Moss made a stupid choice. It's that Moss made the choice a generous, trusting person would make. The dice decided the rest.

The sun is gone. Vecna is free. And Moss almost certainly knows, somewhere in their gut, that they're the one who did it. What they do with that knowledge — whether they carry it, atone for it, or go back to studying fungi and try not to think about it — is the character question that would define any future sessions.

III Earnest Generosity ⌄

Before the catastrophic Insight roll, Moss had already demonstrated what kind of character they were. When Gar lost both legs, Moss reanimated them. This is a strange and creepy thing to do. It also solved the problem completely, kept the mission viable, and let Gar keep his dignity by remaining mobile. Moss didn't ask permission. They assessed the situation, applied their abilities, and fixed what they could fix.

And when reanimation wasn't fast enough, Moss dragged Gar across the jungle floor. No dramatic speech. No resentment. Just: this person cannot move, I can move us both, here we go.

Moss is the character who shows up for people without making it about showing up for people. That generosity is what Vecna exploited. And it's also what makes Moss worth following into any subsequent adventure — because characters who extend trust this readily, and feel the weight of what happens when that trust is betrayed, are the ones who grow.

A GOAT FARMER IN PARADISE
▼

The sand was wrong.

Gar Stonepeak had grown up on mountain scree and frost-hardened clay, the kind of ground that pushed back when you set your weight against it. This Chultian beach had the consistency of something recently dead — warm, loose, yielding underfoot in a way that suggested it would rather not be involved. The air tasted of salt and rot and something floral he couldn’t name, a sweetness that had no business existing this close to a jungle where everything was trying to eat everything else.

He shifted the greataxe across his back and watched the gnome.

Professor Oddswick Fizzleblast stood on a slight rise of packed sand with the posture of a man who had survived a great many expeditions by refusing to acknowledge that any of them were dangerous. He was short in the way that gnomes were always short — not merely lacking height but compressed, as though someone had taken a full-sized person and pushed down until the proportions settled into something more efficient. His field coat bore the insignia of an academic institution Gar had never heard of and probably couldn’t pronounce. His hat bore the accumulated damage of every bad decision he’d ever made in the field. At his elbow, a thin, nervous figure named Niles clutched a leather satchel with both hands and the expression of someone who had already drafted his letter of resignation and was merely waiting for the appropriate postmark.

“The temple,” Fizzleblast announced, gesturing toward the distant tree line with a sheaf of notes that immediately attempted to escape in the coastal breeze, “holds an idol of considerable archaeological significance. Golden. Serpentine. Approximately the size of a large gourd.” He peered over his spectacles at his three contractors. “I trust you’ve all had your inoculations.”

Gar had not. He wasn’t entirely certain what an inoculation was, but the word contained too many syllables to be relevant to his interests. He was here because he’d been promised coin, and Gar had a goat farm back home that wasn’t going to feed itself. The goats needed hay. Hay cost money. Money came from jobs. The job was here. The logic was clean and uncomplicated, which was how Gar preferred his logic.

The other two contractors occupied positions on either side of a scale that Gar himself anchored at the far end. Sable Underhill was halfling-small, dark-cloaked, and possessed the alert stillness of someone perpetually calculating the distance between herself and every exit in any room she entered. She had been introduced to Gar approximately nine minutes ago and had already attempted to pick Fizzleblast’s pocket during the handshake. The gnome had caught her wrist, wagged a finger, and said nothing. Sable had smiled and said, “Right here the whole time, sir,” with the practiced calm of someone for whom getting caught was simply a data point for the next attempt.

Moss was harder to categorize. The wood elf moved with the unhurried patience of someone who considered the jungle a professional courtesy rather than a hazard. His eyes drifted to the tree line the way other people’s eyes drifted to hearth fires — with recognition, with something close to comfort. He wore his armor like an afterthought and carried himself like a man who spent meaningful stretches of his life wearing a different body entirely. During introductions, he had placed one hand against the bark of the nearest palm tree, closed his eyes, and stood that way for several seconds too long.

“I really love fungi,” Moss had said, when asked what brought him to the expedition. “Gar loves fungi too. They’re fun.”

Gar had not been consulted on this. He had not disagreed.

Fizzleblast ran through the particulars with the fussy precision of someone who had been burned by contractors before. He described the deposit system for the canoes. He described the dangers of the Chultian interior in the abstract, careful way of a man who had read about them extensively and had no intention of experiencing them firsthand. He described his own reasons for not accompanying them with the phrase, “I have a condition. Several conditions. Mostly physical. And one or two emotional.” He described the three types of people he’d been forced to work with over the course of his career: “Mercenaries, murderers, and mothers-in-law.” The third category appeared to carry the most weight.

“You are the reason banks were invented,” he told them, with something between affection and genuine accusation, and then he turned to Niles. “The bag.”

Niles produced a small leather pouch — the portable vault where the expedition’s supplementary magical equipment had been stored for transport. He held it out with the ceremony of a man presenting something precious. Then, through some conspiracy of damp fingers, coastal humidity, and the fundamental unreliability of assistants named Niles, he lost his grip.

The bag hit the sand.

The detonation of arcane items that followed was spectacular in the way that all failures of containment are spectacular — sudden, comprehensive, and distributed across a radius wider than anyone would have predicted. A cape of deep burgundy billowed upward in the explosion of released enchantment and draped itself across Gar’s shoulders before he’d processed what was happening. A shimmering cloak skittered into the undergrowth at Sable’s feet, and she had it in hand before anyone saw her move. A pair of gauntlets landed in the wet sand near Moss, who picked them up and examined them with the same unhurried curiosity he applied to everything.

In the silence that followed, Fizzleblast looked at his notes, made a small annotation, and said nothing.

It was the Tyrannosaurus bellowing somewhere in the middle distance that broke the silence instead. The sound came rolling out of the jungle like an avalanche made of lungs, deep enough to feel in the sternum, resonant enough to move the sand beneath their feet. Gar’s hand went to his axe. Moss tilted his head, listening, the way you’d listen to a neighbor calling across a field.

“A dinosaur?” Gar said. He let the statement settle. “Let’s go kill it.”

He meant it. That was the thing about Gar that the others would learn over the next several hours: there was no distance between the thought and the statement, no mediating layer of strategy or self-preservation. Forward was always a reasonable direction. The dinosaur was a problem. Problems were for solving. Gar solved problems.

Fizzleblast retreated to his camp — a shimmer of arcane protection he called the Fizzle Field, patent pending — and wished them luck from within its safety. He had produced a small cot, a stack of notes, and what appeared to be a stuffed bear, and he arranged these with the practiced comfort of a man settling in for a pleasant afternoon while other people risked their lives on his behalf.

The jungle swallowed them inside thirty steps.

THE TEETH AT THE THRESHOLD
▼

The temple had been waiting for a long time.

Gar’s first thought was not a sophisticated one, but it was accurate. The structure rose from the jungle the way old bones surface from turned earth: slowly, incompletely, still half-claimed by the ground that had been trying to reclaim it for centuries. Roots braided through the stone courses of the outer walls. Flowering vines had colonized the decorative reliefs with patient aggression, turning serpent carvings into living things wrapped in green. A pair of enormous stone snakes flanked the entrance, their scales gone soft with lichen and age, their jaws frozen mid-threat.

The mirrors were the second thing he noticed. Large polished discs of silvered metal, positioned at calculated angles along the temple’s approach path, catching whatever light the canopy allowed and redirecting it in crossing beams. The geometry was deliberate and homicidal. The beams intersected at ankle height, chest height, throat height, overlapping in patterns that covered the entrance without visible gap. The math was elegant. The intent was murder.

“Trap,” Sable said, from somewhere near his hip.

“I can see that.”

“Can you see the apes?”

The gorillons came out of the tree line at a run. Three of them — four — it was hard to count things that moved that fast and that wrong. They had been gorillas once, or something descended from gorillas, before whatever intelligence inhabited this place had improved upon the design. They were taller than gorillas had any right to be, broader across the chest, and behind their eyes there was a focused, predatory awareness that ordinary apes did not possess.

Gar stepped forward, because stepping forward was what Gar did, and caught one of the reflected mirror-beams full across the face.

The light struck like a fist made of white heat. His vision bloomed into a featureless nothing — not darkness but its opposite, an obliterating brightness that filled his skull and left no room for anything else. He staggered, swinging blind, the greataxe carving air where enemies might have been. His feet found nothing certain. The world had been replaced by a flat, thrumming whiteness, and somewhere inside it the gorillons were closing.

“I didn’t even notice,” he would say later. “I was just going in a circle of darkness.”

Moss was already there, his hand on Gar’s forearm, murmuring something that smelled of wet bark and new growth. The blindness peeled away like a bandage lifted from a wound, and the world came flooding back — green, bright, and full of very large apes at an uncomfortable distance.

What followed was less a battle than a controlled collapse toward the only direction that mattered: through. The gorillons hit the mirror beams from the jungle side and learned about them at volume — shrieking, staggering, clawing at their own eyes. Sable found the doorway by instinct, or by the particular gift that rogues possessed for knowing where the openings were before anyone told them. She was through and waving before Gar’s vision had fully cleared. Gar seized the rope mechanism for the stone door as they cleared the threshold, setting his weight against it with everything he had. The slab fell into its groove with a finality that settled into the floor like a verdict.

Outside: the impact of heavy bodies against immovable stone. The sound came twice, three times, then stopped. Something larger sniffed the jungle air beyond the door — something considerably larger than a gorillon — and moved away.

Inside: darkness. The specific kind of darkness that underground spaces produce, not merely an absence of light but a positive presence, a thickness in the air that pressed against the eyes and found nothing to work with. The smell of old stone and dust and something mineral, like licking the flat of a blade.

They were in.

WHAT THE FUNGUS REMEMBERED
▼

The antechamber revealed itself in torchlight the way a body reveals itself on a surgeon’s table — in sections, each one more complicated than the last.

The treasure came first. Coins in drifts against the walls, gold and copper and silver mixed together in piles that had accumulated over decades or centuries. Weapons and armor scattered across the floor in poses that suggested their previous owners had set them down in a hurry, or not at all. The accumulated remnants of every previous expedition that had made it this far — Fizzleblast’s predecessors, their ambition reduced to gleaming detritus.

The floor came second. Sable spotted the pressure plates from the doorway, reading them with the same focused attention she’d applied to Fizzleblast’s pockets. Seams in the stone, barely visible, arranged in an irregular grid. Above them, set into wall slots at hip height, the mechanisms: blade assemblies, greatswords mounted on pivots, designed to sweep the space at the level that would catch a human or elf at center mass.

“Five that I can see,” Sable said. “There will be more under the coins.”

Gar stepped on one anyway, because the distance between seeing a trap and processing its implications required a pause that Gar’s legs were not configured to take. The blade that scythed across his shin drew a line of bright pain from knee to ankle. He grunted, looked down at the thin red stripe on his skin, and stepped over the mechanism. He’d had worse from goats. Specifically, from the black one that kicked.

They looted with the methodical care of professionals who understood that the dead had no use for bracers. Sable went up the walls — the boots she’d recovered from Fizzleblast’s scattered equipment gripped vertical stone the way spiders grip glass, letting her walk along the wall above the plates while the others navigated below. It was the correct solution, executed with the quiet efficiency of someone who had spent her career finding ways to avoid using floors.

In the hidden compartment she discovered by running her fingers along the wall at approximately treasure-height, the haul was substantial. A ring that bent light around the wearer. A cloak that did the same with shadow. Her hand closed around both, and for a moment — just a moment — the calculation was visible in her expression. Keep them. Say nothing. The ring was small. No one had seen her open the compartment.

“What? Hey.” She said it to herself, a correction issued from one part of her mind to another, and then she held the ring up for the others to see. It was a near thing, though. Gar could tell.

Moss, meanwhile, had gone still.

He was crouched near the base of the passage wall, his palm pressed flat against the stone, his eyes closed, his face assuming the angular intensity of someone listening to a conversation conducted in a language that didn’t use words. His lips moved once, twice. His fingers spread against the masonry as though trying to feel the temple’s pulse through layers of stone and mortar.

“The mycelium,” he said. “The fungal network. It runs through the whole temple. It’s been here longer than anything else.” He paused, his head tilting at an angle that was not quite natural — the posture of a man channeling something that existed below the level of conscious thought. “The pictures on the wall. The mural. It’s a key. Walk like the crocodile and the man. Both together.”

The mural he indicated showed a procession: a reptile and a humanoid figure in lockstep, moving as one through a corridor. The rhythm was the key. Step together, step apart. Weight distributed in the pattern the temple expected — the synchronized gait of two very different bodies moving in unison.

The portcullis at the passage’s end was the final obstacle: great iron bars flanked by two oversized greatswords set into the lifting mechanism. Moss transformed. The change was quick and unsettling in the way that all shapeshifting was unsettling to witness from the outside — bone and flesh reorganizing with a sound like wet canvas being wrung out, proportions shifting, the elf’s slim frame expanding and flattening into something broad, scaled, and low to the ground. The crocodile that stood in Moss’s place was four feet at the shoulder and eight feet in length, and it gripped one of the greatswords in its jaws with the calm certainty of something that had been built, at the structural level, for leverage.

Gar wrapped both hands around the other blade and set his feet. Together — the man and the reptile, just like the mural — they pulled. The portcullis rose on a groan of ancient iron, and the passage beyond exhaled a breath of air that carried something chemical and sweet.

Something waiting.

THE ROOM THAT BREATHED POISON
▼

The second chamber breathed.

Not metaphorically. Gar felt it the moment they crossed the threshold — a rhythmic pulse in the air, an exhalation from somewhere in the floor mechanism that carried a heavy, cloying sweetness. The gas gathered in the room’s low places like fog settling into a valley, yellowish-green and faintly luminous, waiting with the patience of something that had been doing this work for a very long time.

Mirrors lined the walls here too, large standing discs angled at positions that suggested engineering rather than decoration. The dust on the floor was thick and undisturbed, which should have been a warning. Gar’s boots left prints in it. Moss’s crocodile-form left broad, drag-bellied tracks. Sable’s left nothing at all, which was consistent with everything Gar had observed about her so far.

Sable stepped wrong.

The gas came up fast — not a gentle seep but a pressurized burst from a pipe concealed beneath the stone. It caught her at chest height, and the sweetness was immediately, suffocatingly wrong. Her eyes went glassy. Her knees folded. She went down in a soundless collapse, the breath leaving her in a thin, whistling sigh, and then she was still in the way that unconscious people are still — not resting, not sleeping, but absent from the body that continued to lie on the floor.

Gar’s lungs burned. He’d caught the edge of the plume, and his fingers were going numb, his vision softening at the edges into something warm and inviting that his mind recognized as extremely dangerous. His constitution was something that could be classified as geological — the product of mountain air and goat-trail stamina and a body that had been built for endurance the way a boulder is built for permanence. He breathed the poison, noted that his extremities felt distant and slow, and chose to simply be heavier than the problem until it passed.

Moss was already moving. Still in crocodile form, still low to the floor where the gas hadn’t yet concentrated, he pushed his broad jaw under Sable’s ribs and began pressing down with rhythmic insistence. It was not, by any medical standard, proper resuscitation technique. It was a four-hundred-pound reptile performing chest compressions on a halfling with more enthusiasm than precision. The absurdity of the image — the enormous scaly snout rising and falling against Sable’s leather armor, the crocodile’s tail sweeping the floor in unconscious counterbalance — would have been funny under other circumstances. Under these circumstances, it was the difference between alive and dead.

She coughed. Gasped. Her eyes opened and found themselves staring up at the underside of a crocodile’s jaw, approximately four inches from her face, performing medical procedures.

“Should I uncrocodile?” Moss asked, his voice emerging from the reptile’s throat in a register that shouldn’t have been possible.

Sable stared at him.

“I will uncrocodile,” he decided.

The puzzle took shape as Moss — back in his own body, dusting stone dust from his robes with the unhurried calm of a man who considered the crocodile incident a routine professional outcome — studied the mirror arrangement. The reflective surfaces were angled to redirect light through the room in a specific pattern. Not just any light. The mirrors were calibrated for a particular frequency, a particular source. He had navigated by moonlight and starlight often enough to understand the underlying principle: optics, geometry, the patient business of bending illumination toward a convergence point that would trigger whatever the ancient builders had designed the room to reveal.

Gar moved mirrors when Moss told him to move mirrors. His contribution to puzzle-solving was primarily structural — he could rotate a four-hundred-pound disc of polished metal that would have taken the other two considerable effort and multiple profanities. He didn’t understand the optics. He understood “turn it left” and “stop there” and “a little more” and the quiet satisfaction of being useful in the specific way that his body made him useful.

Moss’s hands found the last adjustment. Two mirrors shifted by fractional degrees, and the reflected beams converged on a point at the room’s center. Something deep in the floor mechanism clicked with the authority of a lock finding its true key. The portcullis to the next chamber ground open, revealing more darkness, more temple, more depth.

Then the constrictor came out of the statue.

It was the size of a hallway. The carved stone serpent in the room’s corner — the one they’d walked past without a second glance, the one with the gemstone eyes that Gar had assumed were decorative — opened its mouth, and what emerged was not stone but muscle. The snake came fast, the rapid unspooling of coils that filled the chamber with scaled flesh and the dry, papery smell of something that had been kept in a very small space for a very long time.

Moss caught the first wrap around his torso. The constriction was immediate and comprehensive — a tightening band of muscle that compressed his ribs with each exhale and made the next inhale shallower than the last. His face went red, then white. Gar heard the sound of breath being squeezed out of a body that wasn’t designed to be squeezed.

Sable went invisible. The ring on her finger drank the light around her, and one moment she was there and the next she was not. Gar didn’t see where she went. He saw what she did: two daggers, buried to the hilt in the snake’s skull, one through each eye, driven with a precision that spoke of either exceptional skill or genuine fury. Both were available to her in that moment.

The snake’s coils loosened. Moss drew a breath that sounded like tearing paper.

They killed it by making the room too expensive a place for the snake to continue living. Gar’s axe opened long wounds in its flank. Sable’s daggers found the gaps between scales that the snake didn’t know it had. The beast thrashed and struck and wrapped and squeezed, and they hit it and cut it and refused to stop, and in the end the snake lay in heavy coils on the stone floor and the chamber was silent except for the ragged sound of three people who had earned their continued breathing.

The chest behind the concealed panel held a catalogue of wonders. An axe that hummed with a hunger Gar could feel through the haft when he lifted it — a berserker’s weapon, built for someone who had already made peace with what anger was for. A staff that radiated warmth like a banked fire. A blade that seemed, improbably, to know which direction luck lived. Books whose pages shimmered with potential. A cloak that whispered of darkness and wings and the freedom of becoming something with no need for the ground.

They distributed these quickly, each taking what called to them, because the temple had taught them that standing still was a luxury they could not afford.

Moss crouched to collect a soil sample from the corner of the room, scraping it into a pouch with academic enthusiasm. He held it up to the torchlight and sniffed it with professional appreciation.

“That’s feces,” Gar said.

Moss looked at the sample. Looked at Gar. Looked at the sample again.

He put it in his pocket anyway.

THE CATHEDRAL OF SLEEPING THINGS
▼

No door announced the final chamber. The corridor simply ended, and the scale of what lay beyond took a long, disorienting moment to register.

The room was a bowl — circular, vast, open to the sky through an aperture far above where a heliographic lamp directed a controlled shaft of light down to a stone altar at the chamber’s center. The light had the quality of something manufactured: too focused, too white, too deliberate. It illuminated the altar with surgical precision and left everything beyond its reach in shadow.

On the altar, beneath that engineered column of light, a Tyrannosaurus Rex lay sleeping.

Gar had never seen one alive. He had heard stories in taverns from southern mercenaries — the kind of stories that grew with each retelling and the quantity of ale consumed during the telling. None of the stories had been adequate. The creature occupied space the way a mountain occupies space: completely, unapologetically, with the implicit suggestion that the space had always belonged to it and everything else was a visitor. Its breathing moved the air in the chamber in slow tidal pulses that lifted dust from the altar’s surface in regular exhalations. Its hide was the color of old leather, scarred and ridged, stretched over a frame of muscle and bone that made Gar — who was, by most standards, an unusually large person — feel like something that could be swallowed without chewing.

Above the sleeping predator, strung on an arch of bone that spanned the space over the altar: the golden idol. Serpentine, gleaming, approximately the size of a large gourd. Exactly as described. Twenty-five feet up. Directly above the largest living thing Gar had ever seen.

“Can I try to talk to it?” he asked.

Moss and Sable both looked at him.

“The dinosaur,” Gar clarified. “Can I try to talk to it?”

There was a pause.

“It’s asleep,” Sable said, with the measured patience of someone choosing her words very carefully. “And it’s a dinosaur.”

“It might be reasonable.”

Around the bowl’s rim, in the shadows beyond the light’s reach, shapes shifted and settled. Hundreds of them — creatures of every variety the Chultian jungle produced, held in check by whatever quality the focused light possessed. Allosauruses curled against the stone walls. Baboons in chattering clusters on the upper ledges. Snakes, birds, things without names. All of them present. All of them alive. All of them one disruption away from being a problem that no amount of hitting could solve.

Then Gar saw the figures on the walkway above.

Six of them. Moving with the coordinated purpose of professionals, positioned along the rim where the temple’s roof met the open sky. A rival expedition — better equipped, more numerous, with a mage among them whose hands were already shaping something in the air. The Red Fangs. They had not come to negotiate.

The first mirror shattered.

Glass and silver rained down from the upper walkway as the rivals began smashing the reflective arrays that maintained the heliographic beam. The shaft of focused light stuttered. Flickered. The shadows crept inward like water filling a depression.

The T-Rex’s eyes opened.

Everything happened at the same time, because that was how violence actually worked — not in sequence, not in turns, but as a single, overwhelming event that the mind could only process in fragments.

The allosauruses surged from the shadows. The baboons screamed down from the walls in waves, small bodies hurtling through the air with a purpose that belied their size. The T-Rex rose with the slow, tectonic inevitability of something that did not need to hurry because nothing in the world was fast enough to matter. Its roar filled the chamber the way water fills a container — completely, pressing against every surface, compressing the air until breathing required effort.

Gar raged.

The anger came the way it always came — not as a loss of control but as a narrowing. The world simplified. Fear fell away, not because he was brave but because the rage didn’t have room for it, the same way a cup full of water has no room for more water. Pain became information rather than experience. The axe in his hands became an extension of something he couldn’t have named but understood in his bones: the conviction that the space between himself and his enemies was a problem, and problems were for solving.

“What doesn’t kill you makes you angry,” he said, to no one, to everyone, to the concept of surviving, and then he was moving.

The first allosaurus came at him from the left, and he met it with a swing that started in his hips and traveled through his shoulders and arms and into the blade with the full, unmoderated commitment of three hundred pounds of Goliath who had decided that this particular allosaurus needed to be somewhere else. The impact traveled back up through his arms and into his spine. The beast screamed — a sound like tearing metal — and went sideways, trailing blood, and Gar was already turning because the second one was behind him.

Somewhere in the chaos, Moss went down.

Gar didn’t see it happen. He heard it — a wet, heavy sound, like a side of meat hitting a butcher’s block, and then a silence where breathing should have been. When he found a gap between enemies wide enough to look through, Moss was on the chamber floor with the second constrictor snake wrapped around his torso, and the elf’s face was the wrong color, and his chest was not moving.

Two breaths. Moss’s body tried to draw breath twice against the snake’s constriction and failed both times, each attempt weaker than the last, and Gar felt something cold settle in his stomach that the rage couldn’t touch — the understanding that someone was dying, right now, on the floor of this chamber, and he was too far away and too surrounded to do anything about it.

Sable materialized behind the snake. She appeared the way she always appeared — suddenly, silently, from a direction the target hadn’t been guarding because the target hadn’t known she existed. Two daggers, driven through the snake’s skull, one through each eye socket, with a force and accuracy that Gar filed away for future reference as something he did not want directed at himself. The snake spasmed. Its coils loosened by degrees. Moss drew a breath that sounded like it hurt.

Then the fireball.

From above — from the Red Fangs’ mage on the walkway, hands finished with whatever architecture of destruction he’d been building — the blast descended like a falling sun. It struck the center of the arena and expanded outward in a sphere of heat and light that consumed oxygen and baboons and the smaller predators in equal measure. The pressure wave slammed into Gar’s chest. His hair singed. His skin drew tight. The Cloak of Elvenkind on Sable’s shoulders blackened and curled and lost whatever enchantment had woven through its fabric.

The arena cleared. Not through any effort of theirs, but because the fireball had done what fireballs do — simplified a complex problem by removing most of the participants. The smaller creatures were gone. The rivals’ mage stood on the walkway above, hands still smoking, wearing the expression of someone who had accomplished something tactically useful and morally questionable in the same gesture.

The T-Rex remained. It was now fully awake, fully enraged, and occupying the same chamber as three very small, very damaged people.

Sable was already climbing. The bone arch that supported the idol rose twenty-five feet above the arena floor, and she went up it with her daggers for purchase, fast, silent, the ring on her finger bending light around her until she was nothing but a shimmer and a scraping sound. The idol was there at the apex — golden, serpentine, heavy with the weight of centuries and the weight of whatever it contained.

She grabbed it. It was heavier than she expected. Too heavy for the cloak’s wings to support. She held it against her chest, crouched on the arch, and looked down at the T-Rex between her and the ground.

“I’m going to be a miserable bat,” she said, to herself, and there was nothing in her voice but the flat acknowledgment of what the next few seconds would require.

Gar grabbed Moss. The druid was conscious — barely — his weight slack in Gar’s arms, his breathing shallow and wrong. The cape on Gar’s shoulders had one trick left in it. The Cape of the Mountebank. One use. One door through dimensions.

“Through any dimensions?” Gar asked, of the cape, of the universe, of whatever intelligence governed the specifics of magical transport. Then he pulled the clasp.

Reality folded. The door opened — not a physical door but an absence, a rectangular nothing that connected here to somewhere else. Gar stepped through with Moss in his arms, and the nothing closed behind them, and the T-Rex’s roar cut off mid-breath as though someone had closed a window.

They landed in the jungle. Not where they’d intended. A hundred feet deeper into the undergrowth than they should have been, surrounded by the close, wet heat of the Chultian forest at dusk, with no line of sight to the temple and no line of sight to the camp.

Sable was still inside.

SIX MINUTES OR YOU ALL DIE
▼

The jungle at dusk was a different country. The light had gone the color of old blood, filtering through the canopy in angles that made every shadow reach farther than it should. The air was weighted with moisture and the smell of organic decay, the jungle processing its dead the way it always did — constantly, without sentiment.

Gar held Moss against his side with one arm. The druid was on his feet, technically, in the way that a scarecrow is on its feet — held upright by the pole rather than by anything resembling structural integrity. They had a hundred feet of jungle between them and Fizzleblast’s camp. In an open field, a minute’s walk. In the Chultian jungle, with the sun failing and something very large following them from the direction of the temple, the distance was a different kind of problem.

Moss pressed his palm against the nearest trunk and the mycelium answered. The fungal network ran beneath everything here — temple, jungle floor, rotting trunks and living roots. It knew which way the water ran. It knew where the ground sloped toward the coast.

“That way,” Moss said, pointing with the certainty of someone whose navigation system was older than cartography. He studied the moss on the trees — his namesake, his people, the growth patterns that told him north from south by the logic of moisture and light exposure. “The camp is there.”

They moved. Gar crashed through undergrowth that parted for him reluctantly, his greataxe clearing the worst of it, his legs driving forward with the grim, metronomic persistence that was his particular gift. Moss navigated. His survival instincts were operating at a level that transcended his injuries — something primal, something beneath the pain and the exhaustion, guiding them through the tangle of green with an accuracy that felt, in its precision, almost supernatural.

Sable was alone in the temple, and the temple was not done with her.

She worked backward through the trap rooms with the focused intensity of someone who had committed to surviving on the basis that dying would be an unacceptable professional outcome. The closing door caught her at the first passage — the heliographic configuration’s failure had started a cascade of mechanisms the builders had designed to seal the complex, and she slid under the descending stone slab with centimeters to spare, the grinding weight of it scraping the top of her pack.

The gas room. She held her breath, picked her line through the pressure plates, and stepped wrong. The blades came out of the wall.

She lost two fingers. The left hand, the middle and ring fingers, taken cleanly by a mechanism that had been designed to remove things cleanly. The pain was a white flash followed by a hot throb that she filed away in the part of her mind reserved for things she would process later, if there was a later.

The blade trap in the corridor took more. She ran through it — there was no other option, no route around, no wall-walking solution when the walls were part of the mechanism — and the blade caught her below the knee and she felt her leg go the way you feel a branch break. A separation. A wrongness. A sudden absence where something had been, and the hot wet rush of blood on the stone floor behind her.

She did not stop. The Cloak of the Bat was still functional, the ring was still on her remaining fingers, and whatever it was that constituted Sable Underhill’s essential self — the compound of stubbornness, professional pride, and the specific refusal to let anything, including the loss of a leg, alter her assessment of whether she was going to survive — remained intact. She went invisible. She used the ring, the cloak, and the pure mathematics of knowing exactly how much she weighed and how much force was needed to hoist a counterweight. She slid through the final gate alongside the Red Fangs who were also exiting the temple, invisible and one-legged, riding their engineering without their knowledge.

She emerged into the jungle missing a leg and two fingers and carrying nothing but her daggers and the kind of determination that frightened reasonable people.

In the jungle, the gorillons found them.

The same ones from the entrance — or their kin, drawn by the noise and the blood and the particular scent of things that were injured and moving slowly through their territory. They came through the undergrowth with purpose, four arms and tusks and the intelligent malice that made them worse than ordinary predators.

Moss tried to calm them. He had a way with animals, usually — the druidic gift for communicating in the register of instinct, the language that predators recognized as neither threat nor prey. He reached out with that gift, and the gorillons looked at him with something that might have been contempt, and did not calm.

The escape was collapsing. Two failures in the gauntlet. The math was simple and the conclusion was ugly.

They settled it the old way — a contest between Gar and Moss, a test of who would press forward and who would stay behind to hold the line. Gar lost. His body, which had carried him through the temple and the T-Rex and the dimension door, chose this moment to announce that it was finished cooperating with his demands. His legs buckled. Not the clean break of Sable’s injury but the systemic failure of muscles and bone that had been asked to do more than they were built for, pushed past their limits and then past the limits past the limits.

Moss dragged him. The druid wrapped both hands around Gar’s armor straps and hauled him through the undergrowth with a strength that shouldn’t have existed in a body that damaged. Gar’s legs left furrows in the jungle floor. The camp shimmered through the trees, close enough to see, far enough to be a cruelty.

The gorillons circled. And then, from behind them — from a direction no one was watching because no one had known there was anything behind them — came a dagger.

It sank into the lead gorillon’s back between the shoulder blades. The beast screamed and spun, and there was nothing there. Nothing visible. Nothing to fight. Just the empty jungle air and, somewhere in that air, the invisible presence of a one-legged halfling who had been inside a death trap for five hours and had reached the absolute end of what she was willing to tolerate.

“What sound do you make, Sable?” the jungle seemed to ask.

“I want to go home,” she said. “Whatever that sounds like.”

It was not a battle cry. It was not a threat. It was the honest vocalization of a person who had lost enough that losing more meant nothing, spoken with a sincerity so raw and so unmanufactured that it bypassed every predatory instinct the gorillons possessed and struck something deeper. Something parental. Something that recognized the sound of a creature that just wanted to go home and remembered that they, too, had left something behind.

The gorillons fled. They turned and ran, crashing through the undergrowth in the direction of their young — their babies, somewhere in the jungle, left unattended while they’d been chasing wounded bipeds through cursed architecture. They went to check on their children.

Gar, face-down in the mud, Moss’s hands still locked on his armor, heard the gorillons leave and felt something in his chest loosen that he hadn’t known was tight. “Hey,” he said, into the dirt. “Gar believes in you. And Gar believes in Gar.”

The final sprint was no sprint at all. Sable — invisible, one-legged, and operating on reserves that existed somewhere beyond exhaustion in a territory that had no name — made the calculation that defined her. She stabbed Gar and Moss in the back. Not to kill. To hold on. Her daggers punched through their packs and into the muscle beneath, and she clung to them as Moss dragged all three of them toward the camp in a final, crawling, desperate surge. Their loot spilled behind them — gold coins, enchanted items, everything they’d bled for, scattering across the jungle floor like a trail leading back to a place they never wanted to see again.

The bubble received them. The Fizzle Field’s membrane parted like water, and they fell through into the warm, humming safety of Fizzleblast’s camp. The gnome was asleep on his cot, his teddy bear tucked against his chest, snoring with the contentment of a man who had delegated all risk to other people and found the arrangement satisfactory.

They collapsed. They breathed. Gar lay on his back and stared at the dome’s translucent surface and listened to the sound of his own heartbeat and thought of goats. The specific goats. The black one that kicked and the grey one that escaped every fence he built and the small brown one that followed him around the farm like a dog. He thought about hay and fence posts and the mountain air that tasted of nothing but cold and distance and home.

“But we survived,” he said. “But we’re alive.”

THE EYE THAT SHOULD NOT HAVE BEEN TOUCHED
▼

The ruby was set into the golden idol’s eye socket — a gem the size of a quail’s egg, deep red, faceted in a way that caught the dome’s faint light and held it.

Moss found it the way he found everything: by touching the surface of something and listening to what it told him. The idol was in his hands — Sable had passed it to him during the final collapse into camp, too exhausted to hold anything but consciousness — and his fingers traced the serpent’s golden coils until they found the eye. The ruby was warm. Not the residual warmth of a body or a fire, but a warmth that came from inside the stone itself, as though something within it was alive.

It whispered.

The voice was not a voice. It was a pressure behind the eyes, a warmth in the chest, a suggestion that arrived in the mind already shaped into meaning. Take me. Take me and your wounds will close. Take me and what was lost will be restored. Take me and live.

Gar watched Moss’s face change. The druid’s expression shifted from exhaustion to attention to something that looked, from the outside, like hope — the specific, dangerous hope of a person who has been offered exactly what they need at the exact moment they need it most.

“It says it can heal us,” Moss said. “The ruby. If I remove it.”

Gar’s legs were finished. Sable’s leg was gone. Her fingers were gone. They were sitting in a magical bubble on a beach at the edge of a jungle that wanted them dead, and outside the bubble the last light was failing, and the ruby was offering salvation.

“Can you trust it?” Gar asked.

Moss tried. Gar could see the effort — the druid reaching for insight the way a man reaches for a handhold on a cliff face, stretching, straining, trying to feel past the warmth and the kindness of the voice to whatever lay beneath. But his mind was fogged with exhaustion and trauma and the accumulated damage of a body that had been a crocodile, a snake, and itself several times over in the span of hours, each transformation taking something that the previous one hadn’t fully repaid.

He got close. Gar could see that too. The flicker of doubt that crossed Moss’s face, the moment where his fingers hesitated on the ruby’s faceted surface. He was close enough to sense that there was more to the voice than it was saying.

Not close enough to hear the name underneath. Not close enough to recognize what was speaking.

Moss pulled the ruby from the idol.

The healing was immediate and total and wrong.

Gar’s legs rebuilt themselves from the stumps outward. New flesh knitting over shattered bone with a warmth that felt like sunlight on cold skin — except the flesh was grey. A degree or two cooler than the rest of him. The muscles worked, the joints articulated, the weight bore, but there was a delay between intention and response, a fraction of a second where the new limbs were translating his commands from a language they did not natively speak.

Sable’s fingers sprouted from the knuckles of her left hand. Her leg rebuilt itself from the stump. She flexed the new fingers and they moved — half a beat slower than the old ones, their grip slightly uncertain, their color the grey of something that had not been alive when it was attached.

Undead flesh. They wore it now. All of them. Patched with the tissue of the dead, walking monuments to the cost of a whispered lie.

Outside the bubble, the stars went out.

Not one by one, the way clouds take them. All at once, like a candle pinched between fingers. The sky became not dark but absent — a void where light had been, filled only by the nothing that exists when everything is removed. The moon vanished. The constellations vanished. The last traces of sunset at the horizon were snuffed as though someone had put a lid on the world.

And in the void, something took shape. A skull. Vast, luminous with a light that was not light but its inverse, a radiance that made the darkness deeper rather than relieving it. The skull’s jaw opened, and what came out was laughter — the laughter of something very old and very patient that had been waiting for exactly this, for a druid to trust the earth, for mercy to become the weapon that undid the only ward standing between a continent and eternal night.

Vecna.

The name arrived in Gar’s mind the way the healing had arrived in his legs — fully formed, already terrible, already too late to matter. The Eye of the Shining Sun. That was what the ruby had been. The only divine ward protecting the continent of Chult from the undead dominion of something that had been watching and whispering and waiting for someone desperate enough to listen.

Moss had listened. Because his friends were dying. Because the earth had always been trustworthy before. Because a god of undeath had said take me and live and Moss, who loved fungi and spoke to trees and believed in the goodness of the natural world with every fiber of his being, had believed it.

The ruby crumbled to ash in his palm.

The idol began to dissolve — the gold tarnishing, flaking, losing cohesion like a sandcastle at high tide. By morning there would be nothing left. Except there would be no morning.

Around the bubble, the jungle came alive with the sound of things that had been dead finding that they were not dead anymore. Bones clattered in the undergrowth — dinosaur skeletons assembling from scattered remains. The gorillons’ screams changed pitch, taking on a hollow quality that had nothing to do with living throats. Somewhere in the darkness, the rival mage team screamed too, but their screams had the specific, truncated character of people who had just stopped being the ones giving orders.

Inside the bubble, Fizzleblast slept on.

He woke in the morning that did not come. Looked at the void where the sky should have been. Looked at the skull that hung where the moon had been. Looked at his three contractors, sitting in the ruins of his expedition with grey limbs and empty packs and the silence of people who understood exactly what they had done and could not undo it.

He looked at his notes for a long moment.

He made an annotation.

Gar Stonepeak stood up. His new legs held. They were not his legs, not entirely, but they held, and that was enough. He crossed the camp in three strides, stood over the sleeping gnome, drew back his fist, and punched Professor Oddswick Fizzleblast squarely in the face.

Fizzleblast’s head rocked back. He blinked once, looked up at Gar with the confused expression of a man woken from a pleasant dream, muttered something incomprehensible, and went back to sleep.

The teddy bear remained undisturbed.

Outside, Vecna’s laughter continued, and the jungle moved with things that no longer needed light to hunt, and the sun did not rise, and would not rise, and somewhere in the void the skull’s empty sockets looked down upon a continent that had been purchased with an act of mercy by a druid who loved the earth and had been answered by something wearing the earth’s voice.

They had retrieved the artifact. They had completed the job. They had survived — technically, in the most pyrrhic sense the word could bear.

Everything else had not.

Fizzleblast would have considered this a mixed result.

In the morning that never came, Moss turned the ash between his fingers and understood, with the terrible clarity that only arrives after the mistake has been made, that the earth he had always trusted had been speaking with someone else’s voice — and he had answered it anyway, because his friends were dying, and that was what trust was for, and trust was the last light over Chult, and the last light was out.
